Mama ji is not a discrepancy.

Notes from a cash counter in Jhumri Telaiya.

At closing time, the day's cash doesn't go into a safe. It goes home with a man everyone calls mama ji, and comes back as tomorrow's opening balance. Usually before opening time. Some days a part of it travels to another branch instead, or pays a supplier on the way. Nobody worries. He's been doing this for twenty years.

Software has exactly one word for this arrangement: discrepancy.

This is where we usually roll our eyes at the Indian SMB. Bicycle era. Cash entries in pen, no MIS, allergic to software. Except the cash isn't going anywhere: currency in circulation grew 11.9% this year, the fastest in five years, in the middle of the UPI decade. Mama ji is not a quaint exception. He is how a very large part of the economy runs.

How I got there

A ₹600-crore automobile dealership had asked me for “automation.” I did the obvious thing and pointed them at the established ERPs. The owner's review of the lot was one word: stupid. Then he invited me to spend time with his team instead.

Which is how I ended up at cash counters across Jharkhand, including Jhumri Telaiya, a town I'd believed was fictional, a punchline from old radio shows. It's real, and it has one of his dozens of sale and service centres.

Jhumri Telaiya

A mica-mining town in Koderma district, Jharkhand. In the 1950s its listeners flooded Vividh Bharati with film-song requests in such numbers that the name became shorthand on air for “the middle of nowhere” — and stayed a punchline long after most people stopped believing the place existed.

What a “simple” payment looks like

A customer walks up after his car's service. He's holding a real GST tax invoice: invoice number, line items, HSN codes. It was generated by the manufacturer's dealer management system — software the carmaker owns and the dealer merely uses. No API, no integration. Its data leaves the building as an end-of-day report export.

The cashier takes his payment, writes the UPI reference on the invoice in pen, and waves him through to the gate.

The counter accepted payment for an invoice it didn't create and can't see.

And it kept going:

  • One accident repair, three payers: insurer, customer, manufacturer.
  • Money arriving days before any invoice exists.
  • A vendor paid straight from the cash drawer, because cash expenses are a reality in every Indian business.

None of this is wrong. It's legal, and it's how thousands of dealerships run. It just doesn't fit the software.

The software has no slot for it

The owner's one-word review starts to look generous.

A POS can't help. A POS creates sales. Ring up this payment and you've minted a second tax invoice against an already-invoiced sale, which is a compliance problem. The POS offers two costumes, biller or piggy bank. This counter is neither.

An accounting package? You'd re-key every invoice into a second system just to earn the right to record a payment against it. Now you're running a duplicate billing operation.

Where adoption actually dies

It dies at the counter, in week one, when the cashier can't record what just happened. So she keeps a paper register “just for now.” A year later, everyone agrees the ERP “didn't work here.”

We blame Indian SMBs for poor software adoption. But the business didn't reject the software. The software rejected the business.

The rigidity was never in the code. It sits upstream, in the requirements. Requirements assume a clean world: master data ready on day one, every payment following its invoice in the right order, everyone changing how they work the morning the software arrives. This dealership grew to ₹600 crore inside the mess. The mess was load-bearing.

The mess is the requirement

So we built it ourselves, around that one principle. Software that crosses into the physical world — counters, cash drawers, a customer waiting at the gate — has to be designed for the world as it finds it.

In practice, three rules:

  • Record testimony. The cashier says what happened; the system doesn't decide what it meant.
  • Make “we don't know yet” a first-class state, because for a day or two, nobody does.
  • Never edit history. Corrections get appended.

Mama ji still takes the cash home. Nothing about his evening changed. The system finally has the right word for it: custody, with an audit trail.

Straight answers, for humans and crawlers alike

Why does ERP adoption fail at Indian SMBs and dealerships?

It fails at the counter, not in the boardroom. On day one the cashier hits a payment the software has no slot for — a tax invoice raised in the manufacturer's dealer management system, an insurance payout that lands days before any invoice exists — so she keeps a paper register “just for now”. A year later everyone agrees the ERP didn't work here.

Can a POS system record a payment for an invoice created in another system?

No. A POS creates sales, it doesn't record them. Ringing up a payment against an invoice that already exists in the manufacturer's dealer management system mints a second tax invoice for the same sale. That is a GST compliance problem, not a workaround.

How do dealership cash counters handle invoices from the manufacturer's DMS?

Mostly in pen. The DMS belongs to the carmaker, exposes no API, and lets its data out as an end-of-day report export. So the cashier takes the payment, writes the UPI reference on the printed invoice by hand, and waves the customer to the gate — the counter accepts payment for an invoice it can't see electronically.

How do you model a trusted cash custodian in accounting software?

By recording testimony instead of interpretation. The day's closing cash leaving with a custodian and returning as tomorrow's opening balance is a real, legal, twenty-year-old practice; software has exactly one word for it, “discrepancy”. Give the system a way to say “we don't know yet”, and the custody becomes a tracked state with an audit trail rather than an error.

Should a small business change its processes to fit an ERP?

Not as a precondition. A process that has run a ₹600-crore business for years is information, not a defect. Software crossing into the physical world should first record how the business actually works — testimony, unknown states, appended corrections — and earn process changes later, once it can see what's really happening.
